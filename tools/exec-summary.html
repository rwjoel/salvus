Section 02

The Partnership Architecture

How the entities are structured, who owns what, and how revenue flows from clients to partners.

Entity Structure

Three Entities, One Firm

Salvus operates through a deliberately layered structure that separates the operating revenue partnership (with TPG) from the ownership governance (among founders and partners). This protects the core partnership from external interference while enabling capital transactions.

The governance firewall: TPG (Purple Marlin) owns 30% of SPM — the revenue-sharing entity — but is permanently excluded from SL24, which holds all ownership governance rights, equity decisions, and partner admissions. SL24 owns SWM (Salvus Wealth Management LLC) 100% — SWM continues as the operating entity after deregistration, serving as the employer of record, payroll processor, HR administrator, benefits provider, and the entity that pays all firm operating expenses. This layered structure ensures complete operational continuity and governance control regardless of the revenue partnership with TPG.

Section 07

Critical Factors & Risk

What must go right, what could go wrong, and where the plan has real vulnerabilities that need attention now.

CRITICAL
Buy-Sell Agreement — Alternative Protection (Life Insurance Likely Unavailable)
Traditional life-insurance-funded buy-sell agreements are likely unavailable to both Bob and Chuck given health history. This means the buy-sell must be structured around alternative protections: (1) disability buyout insurance — underwrite immediately, as this may still be available; (2) a contractual cross-purchase obligation triggered by incapacity, funded by a pre-built firm cash reserve; (3) an accelerated buyout schedule if a triggering health event occurs; and (4) a defined "disability bridge" period where the incapacitated partner's K-1 continues at a reduced level until buyout completes. The attorney must draft explicit disability and death provisions into the SL24 Operating Agreement — this is the most urgent unaddressed structural risk.
CRITICAL
SL24 Operating Agreement — Attorney Review & Finalization
The SL24 OA must be finalized before any 2027 equity transactions occur. Critical provisions still needed: explicit permission for third-party lender financing of equity purchases, Bob's first right of refusal language, Pete's admission criteria pathway, TPG/Purple Marlin permanent exclusion, managing member succession, and the uniform valuation formula. Questions 8–18 from the attorney review remain open.
CRITICAL
O'Quinn Pre-Qualification with Conventional RIA Lender
O'Quinn must be pre-qualified with Oak Street Funding or PPC Loan in 2026 — well before the 2027 close. His personal credit, financial statements, and net worth will be underwritten. If there are issues, there may be 6–12 months to resolve them. Discovering a problem in Q1 2027 when the transaction is ready to close would be catastrophic for the timeline.
IMPORTANT
AUM Retention Through the Transition
The entire financial model assumes minimal client attrition through the ownership transition. In practice, some high-net-worth clients may follow Chuck personally or become uncertain during the change. A 10% revenue decline in 2027–2028 would reduce NOI by ~$200K — still manageable given the 4.4× coverage ratio in those years, but worth monitoring and communicating proactively to key clients.
IMPORTANT
Sonja Elia — Operational Continuity and Succession
Sonja is the operational backbone of the firm. As the transition adds complexity (new partners, new billing systems, Fidelity integration, Addepar reporting), her role becomes more critical — not less. Cross-training, salary review, and a formal key-person retention plan should be in place before the 2027 transition begins. A Sonja departure during the transition would be a significant operational risk.
IMPORTANT
The 2032 Overlap Year — Revenue Sensitivity
In 2032, both buyout obligations run simultaneously: Chuck's final note payment ($462K/yr) and Bob's first full buyout year ($942K/yr). Total: $1.40M against $2.61M NOI — a 1.86× coverage ratio. A severe market correction (−20% AUM) in 2031–2032 could reduce NOI to $1.6–1.8M and compress coverage below 1.3×. A 3–6 month debt service reserve fund, pre-funded from 2030 income, is strongly recommended.
MANAGEABLE
Outstanding Billing and Operational Issues (O'Quinn / Freidel Onboarding)
~13 client accounts with uncollected fees, Schwab NAA not executed, Fidelity billing gaps, and Addepar reporting format issues from advisor onboarding. These are operational, not existential — but must be fully resolved before 2027 so that combined revenue figures used in the buyout valuation are accurate and auditable.
MANAGEABLE
Fee Compression Over Time
The model assumes flat advisory fee rates. Industry-wide, fees are compressing at approximately 0.5 bps/year as clients become more fee-aware and competition from robo-advisors increases. At the current fee level of ~163 bps blended, Salvus has meaningful buffer — but the firm should monitor fee realization annually and offset any compression with AUM growth.
