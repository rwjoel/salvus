Plan B — Independence & the Replacement Capital Partner

A complete contingency to the TPG / Salvus Purple Marlin transaction: bring O'Quinn's and Freidel's clients home to SWM, run operations independently, fund the Woolston buyout without revenue-sale proceeds, and grow toward $1B with a minority capital partner rather than a controlling platform. Built for readiness, not as a decision to leave — so that if the TPG relationship cannot be fixed, you can move with confidence rather than improvise.

The bottom line

Walking away pre-close is clean and contractually feasible. No equity has transferred, so there is nothing to buy back. O'Quinn's IAR agreement explicitly preserves his ownership of client relationships and goodwill, imposes no client non-solicit, and allows exit on 30 days' notice. The clients can come home to SWM.

The hard problem isn't leaving — it's re-funding the Woolston buyout. TPG cash was going to seed it. Without that, the buyout runs on a Chuck seller note plus bank debt plus internal cash flow, and — per your stated preference — a minority growth-capital partner that funds liquidity while you keep control.

This is a readiness document, not a resignation. The TPG deal may yet work. The purpose here is to remove the fear of the unknown from that conversation, so you negotiate — or walk — from strength.

02

Why Plan B is on the table

This is not about valuation or money on the table. The TPG terms were, if anything, rich. It is about execution, communication, and trust in follow-through — the things that determine whether a multi-year partnership actually works.

  • Service and operational failures that the issue tracker now documents in real time — the pattern, not any single item.
  • Communication gaps — relay delays through Home Office, slow acknowledgment, blame-shifting under pressure.
  • Greg leading but appearing oblivious — not taking the issues to heart, not giving them the attention a new partnership demands.
  • The downstream doubt — if execution is this strained pre-close, can Greg be relied on to follow through on every future revenue purchase the deal contemplates? That is the question that turns service problems into a structural risk.

The honest framing

You like Greg personally and the deal economics were generous. Plan B exists because personal regard and good terms don't compensate for doubt about delivery over a five-to-seven-year arc. Readiness here is what lets you raise these concerns candidly with Greg — because you'll be negotiating with a real alternative, not a bluff.

03

Feasibility gate — what the contract actually says

The single question that could have killed Plan B: can O'Quinn and Freidel bring their clients back, or does TPG own them? The executed IAR agreement language settles it decisively in your favor.

Client ownership — retained by the IAR

“ThePARTNERS shall not have any protectable ownership interest in… all existing client relationships as of the effective date… The IAR shall retain exclusive ownership of all the aforementioned client relationships, client lists, contact information, and associated goodwill.”

TPG does not own O'Quinn's book. The relationships, lists, contact data, and goodwill are explicitly his.

No client non-solicit

The only restrictive covenant (¶29) is an employee/advisor non-solicit — it bars poaching TPG's staff, contractors, signal providers, and other advisors for two years. It is silent on clients. And it carves out anyone “whom the IAR originally referred to or introduced to ThePARTNERS, or with whom the IAR has an existing relationship” — which is exactly how O'Quinn and Freidel arrived, through Salvus. They sit inside the exemption.

Exit — 30 days, no cause, no penalty

“If termination is not for cause, it will be effective upon 30 days written notice to the other party.”

Two items to confirm — neither a blocker

1. Signed status. Confirm directly with O'Quinn and Freidel whether the IAR agreements are executed, and have John Suender review the signed versions against this read. If unsigned, they're freer still — no covenants at all.

2. Schwab relationship. The clients are O'Quinn's; the custodial advisor agreement may be TPG's. Most likely the clients repaper onto SWM's own Schwab Advisor Services relationship, or move to Fidelity. Call Schwab to confirm SWM can stand up its own institutional relationship and the mechanics of bulk repapering.

The $1B-by-2031 ambition now runs on internal capacity plus whatever partner you choose. Organic growth, selective tuck-ins (a capital partner can fund these), and the AI-forward operating posture you've already been building become the engine in place of TPG's platform.

05

Funding the Woolston buyout without revenue-sale proceeds

The TPG plan used the additional-10% option (~$2.08M) to seed Chuck's down payment. Remove it, and the buyout must be assembled from four sources — ideally weighted toward the one that preserves your control.

SourceRole in the buyoutTradeoff
Chuck seller noteThe linchpin. Chuck financed by the firm over 5–6 years rather than cash up front. Lowest external dependency.Requires Chuck's willingness to carry paper — the key open question.
Bank acquisition debtOak Street / PPC / Live Oak-style RIA lending against cash flow. Bridges the gap the seller note doesn't cover.Personal guarantee at 59; the opposite of de-risking the family.
Internal cash flowThe retained 7% fee (~$249K/yr) plus margin fund ongoing note payments.Constrains distributions during the payoff window.
Minority capital partnerYour preferred lever. Revenue/earnings-participation capital purpose-built to fund a buyout while you keep control — reduces the debt and the personal guarantee.Introduces an outside economic partner; choose structure carefully (Section 06).

The two questions that size everything

1. Will Chuck carry a 5–6 year seller note? His appetite determines how much external capital you need. A generous note shrinks the debt and may eliminate the need for an outside partner entirely.

2. How much personal debt are you willing to guarantee at 59? The more you'll carry, the more the fully-independent path (debt-only) works. The less you want to carry, the stronger the case for a minority capital partner taking part of the load.

Valuation reality — what the buyout is priced against

Firms your size trade at ~5–9× EBITDA / 2–6× revenue. The independent SWM (post-repatriation, ~$475M combined) gets valued on that basis — which is also the basis for Chuck's buyout price and the O'Quinn/Freidel equity tranches. Lower headline than TPG's 8× revenue, but you own 100% of it and pay no platform fee. Get an independent valuation (see Allen Darby, Section 06) before fixing any internal transaction price.

06

The five strategic paths

Independence is the destination; the question is how you finance growth and the buyout once there. Five paths, evaluated against your stated preference for a minority capital partner that preserves control.

Path A

Fully independent

Debt + Chuck seller note only. 100% control, no outside partner, no liquidity event. Maximum control, maximum personal financial risk.

Path B

Platform partner

Dynasty / Sanctuary / NewEdge-style supported independence. Tech & ops, sometimes transition capital. Watch for brand/control erosion (NewEdge's W-2 Pillar drift).

Path C — preferred

Minority growth-capital partner

Revenue/earnings-participation or non-voting minority capital that funds the buyout and growth while you keep control. Closest to “TPG's money without TPG's behavior.”

Path D

New revenue purchaser

Another 8×-style buyer. Replaces TPG cash but reintroduces exactly the dependency and control-dilution you're leaving. Your inbox is full of these.

Path E — ops/TAMP partner only: outsource back office (Orion / Altruist / GeoWealth) for capacity without equity. No liquidity for the buyout, full control. A complement to A or C, not a standalone answer.

The recommended opening sequence

0. Call Allen Darby (Alaris Advisors) first. Path-agnostic sell-side advisor already in your inbox — gives you the independent valuation you're missing and a read on the whole field, with no obligation.

1. Re-engage Wealth Partners Capital Group (Rocco Sutherland) — your one warm minority-capital relationship.

2. Approach Elevation Point — the only minority partner purpose-built for $200M–$3B, tax-efficient, Emigrant-backed.

3. Use the acquirer inbound (Beacon Pointe, Cerity, Merit, Bluespring, et al.) for price discovery and leverage — not as your path. See the Capital Partner Landscape doc for the full map.

The caution from your own history

You've been down the minority-capital road with Summit and Meridian (Merchant) and walked each time. The industry's open secret — minority investors' operational support often “doesn't match the brochure” — is something you've lived. For any replacement, weight references over pitch decks: ask each firm to show you a firm your size where the support actually materialized, and to put you in touch with that advisor.

The point of all this

Plan B's real value isn't that you'll necessarily use it — it's that having it lets you sit across from Greg and say plainly what's wrong, knowing you have a credible, costed alternative. That conversation either fixes the relationship or clarifies that it can't be fixed. Either outcome is better than proceeding on hope.
