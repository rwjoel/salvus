Section 01

The Verdict

Bank financing is a genuinely compelling addition to the Salvus succession toolkit — but with a critical constraint that changes how it applies. The short answer: it should be central to O'Quinn and Pete financing their purchases, and it may solve the single biggest gap in the plan — funding Bob's exit with cash rather than paper.

✓
Bank Financing Is Worthy — With One Critical Caveat
Specialist RIA lenders — Live Oak, Oak Street Funding, SkyView Partners, and PPC Loan — actively finance internal partner buyouts at Salvus's firm size. The loan goes to the buyer (O'Quinn, Pete, eventually the buyers of Bob's stake), not to Salvus itself. This is transformative: it means Chuck and Bob receive cash at closing rather than holding seller notes for years. The caveat: SBA 7(a) loans cannot fund tranched/partial buyouts — a direct conflict with Salvus's multi-year succession design. Conventional loans from Oak Street, SkyView, or PPC can. Use the right product for each transaction.

Section 04

Pros & Cons — Bank Financing vs. Seller Note

✓ Pros — Bank Financing
●
Chuck and Bob get cash at closing. The single most important benefit. Instead of holding a seller note for 6+ years, Chuck receives a check. Bob, when he exits, does too. No performance risk. No waiting. No exposure to a firm you no longer run.
●
Eliminates counterparty risk for sellers. A seller note is only as good as the firm's future performance. A bank loan transfers that risk to the buyer and the bank — the seller is out clean.
●
O'Quinn and Pete can finance 100%. Oak Street Funding explicitly offers 100% financing for RIA successors — no down payment required. This eliminates the capital constraint that often blocks internal succession.
●
No seller note on Bob's books during retirement. Holding a $6–8M seller note while retired is a liability in disguise — your income depends on people you no longer manage. Bank financing removes this exposure entirely.
●
RIA-specific underwriting understands your business. Live Oak, Oak Street, and SkyView underwrite RIAs as going concerns — they understand AUM retention, fee revenue, and client attrition. They will not require hard collateral that an RIA doesn't have.
●
Installment sale tax benefit still available. Even if Chuck receives cash from the bank loan, the equity transfer can still be structured as an installment sale for tax purposes if the bank's disbursement matches the closing schedule. Coordinate with WJM CPA.
●
2025 is a hot lending market. Live Oak reported record volume in 2025. PPC Loan grew 30–35%. Conditions and lender appetite are currently favorable.
✗ Cons — Bank Financing
●
Higher interest rate — significantly. Our seller note model assumes 5.5%. Bank financing in 2025 is 8–10%+ (SBA avg 9.20%). On a $4M buyout, the extra 3–4% is $120–160K/year in additional interest cost to the buyer. This reduces their K-1 take-home.
●
SBA 7(a) cannot fund tranched succession. This is not a minor issue — it is disqualifying for a significant portion of Salvus's plan. Any tranched or partial equity purchase cannot use SBA financing. Must use conventional or hybrid.
●
Variable rate risk. 98.7% of Live Oak SBA loans carry variable rates. Over a 10-year term, a 200bps rate increase adds $40K+/year to the buyer's obligation. Fixed conventional loans are preferred for predictability.
●
Buyer must personally qualify. O'Quinn and Pete will be underwritten on their personal financial statements, credit history, and net worth. If either has significant personal debt, limited assets, or credit issues, approval is not guaranteed.
●
Covenants may restrict flexibility. Conventional bank loans often include AUM retention minimums, revenue covenants, and change-of-control provisions. These can limit future deal flexibility and require bank consent for additional equity transactions.
●
SBA guarantee fee is an upfront cost. For a $4M SBA loan, guarantee fees can run $50–70K or more. This cost is borne by the buyer but indirectly affects deal economics. Conventional loans avoid this.
●
60–90 day closing timeline. Bank underwriting takes time. The Salvus 2027 transaction calendar is already complex (TPG additional 10%, O'Quinn equity admission, Chuck first tranche). Start the pre-approval process in 2026.

The Recommendation

Salvus Legacy 24 · For Discussion with Counsel & WJM CPA · June 2026

Conventional Bank Financing Should Be Central to the Plan — Not Optional

  • Replace the seller note with conventional bank financing for Chuck's tranched buyout. Use Oak Street Funding or PPC Loan (not SBA — tranching is incompatible with SBA rules). O'Quinn borrows to buy Chuck's tranches; Chuck receives cash at each close. The extra ~$117K/year in interest is a real cost to O'Quinn but is covered by his growing K-1 income and eliminates Chuck's note risk entirely.
  • Use bank financing for Pete's admission. Oak Street's 100% financing option eliminates the need for Pete to source a down payment — and eliminates the seller note Bob and O'Quinn would otherwise hold. Pete's first-year K-1 income at 9.5% (~$200K+) likely covers his annual loan payment with money left over.
  • Keep a small seller note (20%) as a hybrid subordinated piece. Many bank lenders require seller participation as a show of confidence in the firm. A 20% seller note ($820K for Chuck) is manageable and gives the bank the signal it wants while delivering 80% of the proceeds to Chuck as cash.
  • For Bob's exit, pursue a combination financing structure now. Start conversations with Live Oak about their $5M SBA + conventional combination program in 2026 or early 2027 — well before the 2031 exit. This is the most direct path to Bob receiving cash rather than a note. Pre-approval signals from Live Oak also validate Salvus's creditworthiness and firm quality to O'Quinn and Pete as buyers.
  • Have O'Quinn and Pete pre-qualify in 2026. Both need to appear credit-worthy to bank underwriters. Their personal financial statements, net worth, and credit history need to be clean. Start this process early — it may reveal issues that need time to address.
  • Update the SL24 Operating Agreement to accommodate bank financing. The OA needs to explicitly permit equity purchases financed by third-party lenders, including any lender consent-to-transfer provisions that banks may require. The attorney needs this in the agreement before 2027 transactions begin.
  • Do not abandon the seller note entirely — it is the lowest-cost option (5.5% vs. 9%+) and remains valuable as a subordinated piece in a hybrid structure. The hybrid model — 80% bank / 20% seller note — is likely the practical optimum for Chuck's buyout.
