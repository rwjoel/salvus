02

Two funding mechanics — the structure matters more than the name

How the capital comes in determines how much control you keep and how cleanly it funds Chuck's buyout. Four structures dominate; the first is the cleanest fit for a founder who wants liquidity without strings.

Revenue / earnings participation

Kudu model · cleanest fit

Investor takes a contractual share of revenue or earnings, stays fully passive, and the capital is permanent. No board seat, no consent rights, no liquidity clock. Purpose-built to fund generational transfers and management buyouts while principals keep control. Ideal for the Woolston buyout.

Non-voting minority equity

Emigrant model

Minority equity, explicitly non-voting, permanent. Heritage in turnkey succession financing — financing next-gen partners to buy out seniors. Preserves independence; "not a control partner."

Preferred minority equity + conversion

Constellation model · most strings

Preferred, structurally senior to common, with put/call rights in year 5+, conversion into 10–40% fully-diluted ownership, and consent rights on major actions. More PE-like; introduces a liquidity clock and governance rights even at minority ownership.

Minority equity + senior debt facility

Elevation Point / Emigrant-backed

Equity stake paired with a debt line for acquisitions and buyouts. Often structured so the initial stake is taxed as long-term capital gains — roughly half the tax of funding the same buyout with a loan.

The caution that applies directly to you

The industry's open secret — and your lived experience with Summit and Meridian — is that minority investors' operational support often “doesn't match the brochure.” If the reason you're leaving TPG is service and follow-through, the single most important diligence question for any replacement is: can they show you a reference firm your size where the support actually materialized? Weight references over pitch decks. And remember — taking minority capital generally commits you to future investment transactions, so size up the whole relationship, not just the first check.
